Extending Multi-Scale Information Supervenience (MIS) Theory with Computational Equivalence, Pockets of Computability, and Effective Information

Abstract

The Multi-Scale Information Supervenience (MIS) Theory provides a formal framework for understanding information flow across quantum and classical scales, addressing the emergent behaviors that arise as systems transition between these levels. While MIS effectively models the relationship between fine-grained (quantum) and coarse-grained (classical) information, it lacks a formal mechanism for characterizing computability within these emergent systems. This paper extends MIS Theory by incorporating Wolfram’s Principle of Computational Equivalence and the notion of pockets of computability, alongside Erik Hoel’s Effective Information (EI), which quantifies the causal strength and computability of systems at different scales. By integrating these concepts, we provide a more detailed understanding of coarse-graining, emergence, and the role of the observer in identifying computable regions within complex informational landscapes.

1. Introduction

Multi-Scale Information Supervenience (MIS) Theory presents a rigorous approach to modeling how information changes across scales, focusing on the transition from quantum to classical systems. The theory addresses how emergent macroscopic phenomena supervene on, but are not reducible to, their quantum counterparts, emphasizing the coarse-graining process that simplifies quantum complexity into manageable classical information.

However, MIS Theory does not explicitly address how computability plays a role in this transition. This paper introduces new tools for understanding how coarse-grained, emergent systems are not just informationally simplified but also computationally tractable. By incorporating Stephen Wolfram’s Principle of Computational Equivalence and his idea of pockets of computability, as well as Erik Hoel’s Effective Information (EI), we aim to enrich MIS Theory’s treatment of emergence and coarse-graining. These additions will provide a more formal mechanism for identifying where and when computability arises within the informational landscape described by MIS.

2. Computational Equivalence and Pockets of Computability

2.1 Wolfram's Principle of Computational Equivalence

Wolfram’s Principle of Computational Equivalence asserts that most systems in nature, when sufficiently complex, are capable of universal computation. This principle suggests that beyond a certain threshold of complexity, the behavior of systems becomes computationally irreducible, meaning that their evolution cannot be simplified or predicted without running through every step of the process. However, Wolfram also acknowledges the existence of pockets of computability—localized regions within a complex system where the behavior is simpler and more computationally tractable.

2.2. Pockets of Computability in MIS Theory

MIS Theory describes how information flows and transforms as we move between quantum and classical scales. By applying Wolfram's pockets of computability to this framework, we can understand macro-level systems as being more computationally accessible than their micro-level, quantum counterparts.

  • Micro-level complexity: At the quantum scale, systems are dense with information and often computationally irreducible. Predicting their behavior requires tracking a vast number of interactions, making them computationally complex.
  • Macro-level computability: Coarse-grained, macro-level systems, on the other hand, provide simpler, emergent properties (e.g., temperature, pressure), which are computable in the sense that their behavior can be described and predicted with less informational complexity.

Thus, coarse-graining can be viewed as a process that reveals pockets of computability within the broader, complex system. The macro-level properties that emerge are not just informationally simplified but are also computationally tractable, allowing the observer to model and predict their behavior without needing to compute every underlying quantum interaction.

3. Effective Information and Causal Strength

3.1 Effective Information (EI) as a Measure of Computability

Erik Hoel’s Effective Information (EI) provides a formal tool for quantifying causal strength within a system. EI measures how much the state of one variable (X) influences the state of another variable (Y) through interventions. A system with high EI has strong causal relationships, meaning that its behavior is highly deterministic and predictable.

In the context of MIS Theory, EI offers a way to measure computability within the system:

  • A system with high EI is computable because its causal relationships are strong and interventions lead to deterministic outcomes. The system’s behavior is tractable and can be predicted without needing to run through the entire complexity of the underlying micro-level details.
  • A system with low EI, on the other hand, has weak causal relationships, making its behavior harder to compute. In such cases, interventions do not lead to clear, deterministic outcomes, and the system’s complexity remains irreducible.

3.2 Coarse-Graining as Maximizing EI

Incorporating EI into MIS Theory allows us to view coarse-graining as the process of finding the scale at which EI is maximized. The macro-level properties that emerge through coarse-graining are more computable because they exhibit stronger causal relationships than the micro-level details, making them deterministic and easier to predict.

4. An Enriched View of Coarse-Graining and Computability

By integrating Effective Information (EI) and pockets of computability into MIS Theory, we gain a more nuanced understanding of how and where computability arises:

  • Coarse-graining as a search for computability: The process of coarse-graining can be understood as the observer’s attempt to identify regions in the system where EI is maximized and the system becomes computable. These are the pockets of computability in Wolfram’s sense, where the system's behavior is deterministic and tractable.
  • EI as a measure of emergent computability: Emergence can now be described not just in terms of informational supervenience, but in terms of computability. The macro-level systems that emerge through coarse-graining are computable pockets within an otherwise irreducibly complex micro-level landscape. These systems have high EI, meaning their causal structure is clear and strong, allowing the observer to predict their behavior with minimal complexity.

5. Conclusion

This paper extends MIS Theory by integrating Wolfram’s Principle of Computational Equivalence, pockets of computability, and Erik Hoel’s Effective Information (EI). These concepts allow us to formalize the process of coarse-graining as a search for computability within a complex informational landscape. EI provides a metric for determining when and where emergent, macro-level systems are computable, while pockets of computability describe the regions where these systems become tractable. This enriched view of MIS Theory offers a deeper understanding of emergence, computability, and the observer’s role in identifying computable structures within complex systems.
