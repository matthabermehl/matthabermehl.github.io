Extending Multi-Scale Information Supervenience Theory with Computational Equivalence, Pockets of Computability, and Effective Information

Matthew Habermehl
September 2024

Abstract

The Multi-Scale Information Supervenience (MIS) Theory provides a formal framework for understanding information flow across quantum and classical scales, addressing the emergent behaviors that arise as systems transition between these levels. While MIS Theory effectively models the relationship between fine-grained (quantum) and coarse-grained (classical) information, it lacks a formal mechanism for characterizing computability within these emergent systems. This paper extends MIS Theory by incorporating Wolfram’s Principle of Computational Equivalence and the notion of pockets of computability, alongside Erik Hoel’s Effective Information (EI), which quantifies the causal strength and computability of systems at different scales. By integrating these concepts, we provide a more detailed understanding of coarse-graining, emergence, and the role of the observer in identifying computable regions within complex informational landscapes.

1. Introduction

The Multi-Scale Information Supervenience (MIS) Theory offers a robust framework for modeling how information behaves and transforms across different physical scales, particularly emphasizing the transition from quantum to classical systems. It addresses how emergent macroscopic phenomena supervene on, but are not reducible to, their quantum counterparts, highlighting the process of coarse-graining that simplifies quantum complexity into manageable classical information.

However, MIS Theory does not explicitly address how computability plays a role in this transition. This paper introduces new tools for understanding how coarse-grained, emergent systems are not just informationally simplified but also computationally tractable. By incorporating Stephen Wolfram’s Principle of Computational Equivalence and his concept of pockets of computability, as well as Erik Hoel’s Effective Information (EI), we aim to enrich MIS Theory’s treatment of emergence and coarse-graining. These additions will provide a formal mechanism for identifying where and when computability arises within the informational landscape described by MIS Theory.

2. Computational Equivalence and Pockets of Computability

2.1. Wolfram's Principle of Computational Equivalence

Stephen Wolfram’s Principle of Computational Equivalence posits that almost all processes that are not obviously simple can be viewed as computations of equivalent sophistication. Specifically, beyond a certain threshold of complexity, systems exhibit computational behaviors that are universal, meaning they are capable of emulating any other computation. This principle suggests that many natural systems are inherently computationally irreducible; their behavior cannot be predicted without simulating each computational step.

2.2. Pockets of Computability in Complex Systems

Despite the prevalence of computational irreducibility, Wolfram also introduces the concept of pockets of computability—localized regions within a complex system where the behavior is simpler and more computationally tractable. In these regions, patterns emerge that can be described and predicted without simulating every underlying interaction.

In the context of MIS Theory, this concept implies that while the microscopic quantum realm may be computationally irreducible, coarse-graining can reveal macroscopic properties that reside within pockets of computability. These emergent properties are more amenable to analysis and prediction, providing practical utility in understanding complex systems.

2.3. Formalizing Computational Equivalence in MIS Theory

To integrate Wolfram's principles into MIS Theory, we consider the computational complexity of the transformations between scales. Let us define:

  • Computational Complexity at Scale \( i \): \( C(\rho_i) \), representing the resources required to simulate or predict the state \( \rho_i \).
  • Computational Equivalence Threshold: A complexity level beyond which systems are computationally irreducible.

The process of coarse-graining from scale \( i \) to \( j \) via the CPTP map \( \Lambda_{i \rightarrow j} \) can reduce computational complexity:

\[ C(\rho_j) \ll C(\rho_i) \]

This reduction occurs because \( \rho_j \) captures emergent properties that reside within pockets of computability, making the system's behavior at scale \( j \) more accessible to analysis.

3. Effective Information and Causal Strength

3.1. Erik Hoel’s Effective Information (EI)

Effective Information (EI) is a measure introduced by Erik Hoel to quantify the causal relationships within a system. EI is defined as the mutual information between the interventions on a system's causal variables and the system's resulting states. Formally, for a system with variables \( X \) and \( Y \), the EI is:

\[ \text{EI} = I(\text{Interventions on } X ; Y) \]

This measure captures how much the system's outputs are determined by its inputs, reflecting the strength of causation and, by extension, the system's computability.

3.2. Incorporating EI into MIS Theory

In MIS Theory, we can use EI to assess the causal strength at different scales. At the macroscopic scale \( j \), the coarse-grained state \( \rho_j \) may exhibit higher EI compared to the microscopic state \( \rho_i \), due to the aggregation of variables and the suppression of microscopic noise.

Let us define:

  • EI at Scale \( i \): \( \text{EI}_i = I(\text{Interventions on } X_i ; Y_i) \)
  • EI at Scale \( j \): \( \text{EI}_j = I(\text{Interventions on } X_j ; Y_j) \)

Typically, we find that:

\[ \text{EI}_j > \text{EI}_i \]

This inequality suggests that the macroscopic scale has stronger causal relationships and is thus more computationally tractable.

3.3. Causal Emergence through Coarse-Graining

The increase in EI through coarse-graining indicates a phenomenon known as causal emergence. By aggregating variables and eliminating microscopic details that contribute to randomness or unpredictability, the coarse-grained system exhibits stronger effective causal relationships.

In MIS Theory, causal emergence can be formalized by considering the EI difference:

\[ \Delta \text{EI} = \text{EI}_j - \text{EI}_i \]

A positive \( \Delta \text{EI} \) signifies that the macroscopic description provides more effective information, enhancing our ability to predict and understand the system's behavior.

4. Enhanced Framework of MIS Theory

4.1. Integrating Computational Concepts into MIS Axioms

We now revisit the fundamental axioms of MIS Theory to incorporate computational equivalence and effective information, ensuring conceptual consistency and a more comprehensive framework.

Axiom 1 (Revised): Scale-Dependent Information and Computability

Statement: The total information content and computational complexity of a physical system can be decomposed into contributions from different scales, each characterized by specific quantum information measures and computational properties that evolve according to scale-dependent dynamics.

The computational complexity at each scale \( i \) is given by \( C(\rho_i) \). The process of moving to a coarser scale \( j \) via coarse-graining reduces this complexity:

\[ C(\rho_j) \leq C(\rho_i) \]

This reduction is due to the emergence of pockets of computability at the macroscopic scale.

Axiom 2 (Revised): Coarse-Graining, Information Loss, and Computational Simplification

Statement: Coarse-graining operations result in the loss of microscopic information and computational complexity, leading to simplified models that reside within pockets of computability.

The increase in entropy \( \Delta S_{i \rightarrow j} \) corresponds to a decrease in computational complexity:

\[ \Delta C = C(\rho_i) - C(\rho_j) \geq 0 \]

This reflects the simplification achieved through coarse-graining, making the system at scale \( j \) more computationally accessible.

Axiom 3 (Revised): Emergence, Reconstruction, and Effective Information

Statement: A macroscopic phenomenon is emergent if there exists no physically realizable reconstruction map that can fully recover the microscopic information, and if the effective information at the macroscopic scale exceeds that at the microscopic scale.

The reconstruction error \( \epsilon_{i \leftarrow j} \) and the increase in EI are linked:

\[ \epsilon_{i \leftarrow j} > 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \Delta \text{EI} > 0 \]

This indicates that while detailed microscopic information is lost (preventing perfect reconstruction), the macroscopic system gains stronger causal relationships, enhancing its computability.

Axiom 4 (Revised): Category-Theoretic Scale Transitions and Computational Structures

Statement: Scale transitions can be modeled as functors between categories representing different computational structures, preserving the mathematical and computational properties relevant at each scale.

The functor \( F_{i \rightarrow j} \) now maps not only the mathematical structures but also the computational aspects, ensuring that computational simplifications are captured in the transition between categories.

Axiom 5 (Revised): Information Conservation, Memory Effects, and Computational Irreducibility

Statement: The dynamics of information in a physical system are governed by conservation laws that include non-Markovian memory effects and computational irreducibility at the microscopic scale, leading to computational simplification at the macroscopic scale.

The computational irreducibility at the microscopic level is reflected in the complexity of the memory functional \( M(t) \). Coarse-graining reduces this complexity, leading to more tractable models.

4.2. Implications for MIS Theory

By integrating computational equivalence and effective information into MIS Theory, we achieve:

  • A unified view of information and computation: Recognizing that information measures and computational complexity are intertwined across scales.
  • A formalism for causal emergence: Quantifying how coarse-graining enhances effective information and leads to emergent computable structures.
  • An explanation for the observer's role: Understanding that the identification of pockets of computability depends on the observer's choice of scale and coarse-graining methods.

5. Applications and Examples

5.1. Quantum to Classical Transition with Computability

System: A quantum system exhibiting chaotic behavior at the microscopic scale but displaying regular, predictable patterns at the macroscopic scale.

Analysis:

  • Microscopic Scale (\( \mathcal{H}_i \)): The system's state \( \rho_i \) is computationally irreducible due to quantum chaos.
  • Macroscopic Scale (\( \Gamma_j \)): Coarse-grained state \( \rho_j \) reveals regular patterns that are computationally tractable.

Effective Information:

At the macroscopic scale, the EI is higher:

\[ \text{EI}_j > \text{EI}_i \]

This indicates stronger causal relationships and enhanced predictability.

5.2. Cellular Automata and Pockets of Computability

System: A cellular automaton (e.g., Rule 110) known for its computational universality and complex behavior.

Analysis:

  • Microscopic Dynamics: The local update rules lead to computational irreducibility.
  • Macroscopic Patterns: Coarse-graining can reveal repeating patterns or structures that are computationally simpler.

Pockets of Computability:

By identifying and focusing on these macroscopic patterns, we can make predictions about the system's behavior without simulating every microscopic interaction.

6. Discussion and Future Directions

6.1. Implications for Complexity Science

The enhanced MIS Theory bridges the gap between information theory and computational complexity, offering new insights into complex systems. It provides a framework for understanding how emergent phenomena can be both informationally rich and computationally accessible.

6.2. Observer-Dependent Computability

The identification of pockets of computability is inherently observer-dependent. Different coarse-graining methods may reveal different computable structures. This highlights the role of the observer in selecting the appropriate scale and modeling approach.

6.3. Extensions to Other Fields

Potential applications extend beyond physics to fields such as:

  • Neuroscience: Understanding how coarse-graining neural activity leads to computationally tractable models of cognition.
  • Economics: Modeling market behaviors where macroeconomic indicators provide computable insights despite underlying complexities.
  • Ecology: Studying ecosystems where emergent patterns (e.g., population dynamics) are more predictable than individual interactions.

7. Conclusion

By integrating Stephen Wolfram's Principle of Computational Equivalence and Erik Hoel's Effective Information into the Multi-Scale Information Supervenience Theory, we have developed a more comprehensive framework that accounts for both informational and computational aspects of emergence. This enriched theory provides a deeper understanding of how coarse-graining not only simplifies information but also enhances computability by revealing pockets of computability within complex systems.

This work opens new avenues for research in complex systems, offering tools to identify and exploit computationally tractable structures. It underscores the importance of scale, coarse-graining methods, and the observer's role in shaping our understanding of the physical world.
